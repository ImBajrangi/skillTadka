Chapter 2: Wave Functions

Section 2.1 - The Schrödinger Equation

In quantum mechanics, the state of a physical system is identified with a vector in a complex Hilbert space. This vector is often represented by a wave function, denoted by the Greek letter Psi (Ψ). The wave function contains all the information that can be known about the system.

The time evolution of the wave function is given by the Schrödinger equation:

iℏ ∂/∂t Ψ(r,t) = Ĥ Ψ(r,t)

Here, Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator, which represents the total energy of the system. Unlike classical mechanics, where position and momentum can be determined simultaneously with arbitrary precision, quantum mechanics imposes fundamental limits on measurement accuracy, known as the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle.

2.1.1 Probability Density

The square of the absolute value of the wave function, |Ψ(r,t)|², is interpreted as a probability density. Specifically, the probability of finding a particle within a volume element dV centered at position r at time t is given by:

P(r,t) dV = |Ψ(r,t)|² dV

This probabilistic interpretation marks a significant departure from the determinism of classical physics. It implies that identical experiments prepared in identical states can yield different outcomes, distributed according to the probability law determined by the wave function.
